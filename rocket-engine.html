ROCKET ENGINE

Design, Manufacturing & Testing Phase

Project Overview

Developed for the Regeneratively Cooled Engine (RCE) Challenge, this liquid-propellant engine is designed to produce 800 lbf of thrust for a 90-second burn duration. The assembly features a high-conductivity copper combustion chamber liner to optimize heat transfer, while the structural airframe and components are machined from aluminum.

1. Manufacturing and design changes

The manufacturing of the 9-inch copper liner was divided into three distinct operational setups. The primary challenge was the internal profiling, which required boring to a depth of 7.9 inches with a minimum bore diameter of 1.17 inches.

2. Manufacturing Process

Internal Profiling & Tooling Constraints

Initial process planning focused on utilizing existing shop inventory with a custom tool holder extension. However, this approach lacked the necessary rigidity for the required depth-to-diameter ratio. To resolve this, the following steps were taken:

  • Tooling Selection: A 0.75-inch solid carbide boring bar was procured to maximize the modulus of elasticity and minimize deflection.
  • Design for Manufacturability (DfM) Adjustment: Through iterative testing, the stable reach limit of the carbide bar was identified at 7.3 inches before the onset of significant harmonic chatter.
  • Optimization: The internal geometry was truncated by 0.6 inches to accommodate the 7.3-inch tool limit. While this resulted in a negligible loss in theoretical performance, it ensured the mechanical integrity and surface finish of the combustion chamber.

Machining & Fixturing Strategy

The manufacturing was split across two primary platforms to leverage specific machine strengths:

  • Primary Profiling (Haas TL-1): The internal and external profiles were turned on a CNC toolroom lathe. To maintain concentricity during these operations, a custom machining mandrel was developed. This fixture utilized tapered locating cones to secure the part between centers, providing the necessary radial support for the thin-walled copper.
  • Feature Machining (Haas VF-2): The 50 complex cooling channels were machined using a 3-vertical axis mill. The previously developed mandrel served as the primary work-holding fixture here as well, ensuring that the external grooves remained perfectly clocked and concentric to the internal bore.
